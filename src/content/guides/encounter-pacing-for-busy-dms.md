Encounter pacing is not only about challenge rating. It is about when the table gets to breathe and when it feels the walls closing in.

## Use different jobs for different fights

Not every encounter needs to prove the same thing. One fight can drain resources, another can reveal a monster habit, and a third can force a hard movement choice. When every combat aims for "epic," the session starts sounding like one long shout.

## Let small fights do small work

A short ambush or guard skirmish can still matter if it changes the party's position, time, or information. Treating every fight like a boss scene wastes prep and teaches players to expect the same emotional volume every hour.

## Count pressure beyond hit points

Pacing gets better when the players are tracking more than damage:

- time before a ritual finishes
- noise that could pull reinforcements
- civilians who need covering
- terrain that gets worse every round

Those pressures create motion. They also give the martial characters more to do than stand in place and roll again.

## Put recovery in the schedule on purpose

If you want a hard day, be honest about it in the world. Safe rest should feel earned. If you want a looser adventure, build clear pauses so support characters are not punished for existing.

## End the session on a useful edge

The best pacing tool is often where you stop. End after a reveal, a clean victory, or the moment before a dangerous room opens. That gives your next session direction before anyone has time to drift.
