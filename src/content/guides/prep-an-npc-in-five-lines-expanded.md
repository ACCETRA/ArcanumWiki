Most NPCs do not need a page. They need a shape the DM can remember while people are asking questions at speed. The goal of NPC prep is not completeness. It is usability. A single line that you can read in half a second and know how to play the character is worth more than a three-paragraph backstory that you cannot access at the table.

This guide presents multiple frameworks for creating NPCs at different levels of detail, from the five-line method for minor characters to more extensive structures for major recurring figures. All frameworks are designed for improvisation under pressure, not for writing exercises during prep.

---

## 1. The Five Lines (Minor NPCs)

The five-line method is for NPCs who matter for one to three sessions. It is the minimum viable prep. If the NPC survives first contact and keeps appearing, you can grow the notes under the same headings.

### 1.1 The Five Lines Structure

Write only these:

1. **What they want right now.** A clear immediate want makes the character active. "Wants to sell his surplus grain before the rainy season ruins it" is more playable than "is a farmer." The want gives the NPC a reason to talk to the party, ask for help, or resist interference.

2. **What they are hiding.** A hidden pressure keeps the character from sounding flat. It does not need to be a dark secret. "Hiding that his crop failed and he is desperate" is enough. The hidden element creates tension when the party gets close to discovering it.

3. **What they sound like.** A vocal habit makes them memorable. This can be a physical voice quality (raspy, too loud, whispers), a speech pattern (speaks in questions, never uses contractions, repeats the last word of sentences), or a verbal tic (ends statements with "you see," clicks tongue while thinking).

4. **What they can offer.** An offer gives the scene a reason to exist. If the NPC cannot give the party something they might want (information, goods, access, aid), the scene is a waste of time. Know what the NPC has and what they would trade it for.

5. **What makes dealing with them difficult.** A difficulty stops them from becoming a vending machine. Maybe they are suspicious of outsiders, bound by a previous agreement, being watched, or simply unpleasant to deal with. Without difficulty, every transaction is too easy and nothing is memorable.

### 1.2 Example: The Five-Line Application

**Tira, the Dockside Merchant**
1. Wants to move a shipment of questionable spices before the harbor inspectors do their weekly rounds.
2. Hiding that the spices are from a ship that arrived with no manifest, and she suspects they might be cursed.
3. Speaks rapidly, sentences running together, as if worried someone will interrupt before she finishes.
4. Can offer passage on a ship she has influence with, and knows which guards accept bribes.
5. Difficult because she insists on doing business in public places where she can be seen, and changes meeting locations at the last minute.

You can run Tira from these five lines. You know what she wants, what she is worried about, how she talks, what she has to trade, and what makes working with her interesting. That is enough to survive first contact and most follow-up interactions.

## 2. The Three-Line NPC Method (Rapid Creation)

The 3-Line NPC method, developed by Johnn Four at RoleplayingTips.com, is designed for creating playable NPC cores in under a minute. It prioritizes speed and table usability over depth.

### 2.1 The Three Lines Structure

**Line 1: Appearance - What Players Can See**

Describe the NPC in a few words, focusing only on what the PCs would actually perceive. Note three interesting things. Use single words where possible. The standard array is [Age, Gender, Culture, Race, Class] plus three notable details.

Example: "[Young Male Deep Dwarf Miner] +1 worn pickaxe, pet weasel on shoulder, permanent sneer."

This line packs in a lot of information fast. The standard array covers the basics. The three details give you personality, equipment, and attitude. From this, you can extrapolate a description at the table.

**Line 2: Behavior - How They Act**

One sentence describing how this NPC behaves in their default state. Are they aggressive, nervous, friendly, suspicious, helpful, detached? This is your default mode for playing them.

Example: "Aggressively friendly to potential customers, becomes sullen and evasive when asked about his past."

**Line 3: Hook - Why the Party Cares**

One sentence about what connects this NPC to the party or the current situation. Without a hook, the NPC is scenery. With a hook, they are a potential story element.

Example: "Owes money to the guard captain and will do almost anything to clear his debt, including betraying confidences."

### 2.2 When to Use the Three-Line Method

Use the three-line method when:
- You need an NPC during play that you did not prep
- You are creating a large cast of minor characters (a village, a ship's crew, a faction)
- You want to test whether an NPC will earn more attention before investing significant prep time

The philosophy is minimum viable product GMing. Do not over-plan or over-prepare. Test your NPC first for "campaign acceptance" before spending a lot of time on them.

## 3. The Drive/Threat/Dilemma/Conflict Framework (Major NPCs)

For NPCs who will recur throughout a campaign, the five-line method is not enough. The Drive/Threat/Dilemma/Conflict framework, developed and documented at thebardicinquiry.com, creates NPCs who feel dynamic and provide multiple threads for players and GM to follow.

### 3.1 The Four Elements

**Drive:** What the NPC actively wants. This is similar to the five-line "want" but should be more ambitious and long-term. Drives should create intersection with the party's goals, either through alignment or opposition.

Examples of strong drives:
- "Restore their family's lost honor"
- "Control all trade through the northern pass"
- "Prevent the prophecy they believe will destroy their people"
- "Become immortal by any means necessary"
- "Protect their child from a destiny they cannot reveal"

**Threat:** What endangers the NPC's drive. A threat without a drive is just danger. A drive without a threat is just a wish. The combination creates motion.

Examples of threats:
- "Their family's ancient enemy has returned with evidence of past crimes"
- "A rival consortium is undercutting their prices with slave labor"
- "The prophecy is coming true faster than they expected"
- "Their immortality ritual requires a sacrifice they swore never to make"
- "Their child is beginning to manifest powers that attract attention"

**Dilemma:** An impossible choice the NPC faces. Dilemmas make NPCs feel human because they force compromise. An NPC without a dilemma is predictable. An NPC with a dilemma is sympathetic.

Examples of dilemmas:
- "They can save their family or their city, but not both"
- "They can expose their rival's crimes, but the revelation would also damage their own operation"
- "They can prevent the prophecy by betraying someone they love"
- "They can achieve immortality but must abandon their humanity"
- "They can protect their child by handing them over to the very people they distrust most"

**Conflict:** What puts the NPC in opposition to the party (or in complex alliance with them). Conflict is not necessarily combat. It is tension. The most interesting NPCs have conflicts that shift over time based on circumstances.

Examples of conflict:
- "The party needs something the NPC cannot give without jeopardizing their drive"
- "The party's actions, however well-intentioned, advance the threat against the NPC"
- "The NPC and the party want the same thing for different reasons"
- "The NPC must choose between helping the party and protecting their own interests"

### 3.2 Example: Drive/Threat/Dilemma/Conflict

**Seraphina Dusk, Captain of the Wardens**
- **Drive:** To maintain order in a city where order is failing
- **Threat:** The criminal guilds have united under a single leader who cannot be bought or intimidated
- **Dilemma:** She can save the city by invoking martial law, but doing so betrays the principles she has spent her life defending
- **Conflict:** The party has evidence that would help her stop the guilds, but using it would reveal the party's own illegal activities

Seraphina is playable from this framework. You know what she wants, what endangers it, what impossible choice she faces, and why interacting with her is complicated. Every scene with Seraphina has built-in tension because of the dilemma and conflict.

## 4. The Purpose Framework (Antagonists and Allies)

For NPCs who serve a specific function in the campaign, the Grumpy Wizard blog's Purpose framework provides clarity about role and implementation.

### 4.1 Purpose-Driven NPC Creation

**Step 1: Define the purpose.** Is this NPC a villain, an ally, a resource, a source of verisimilitude, or an information source? The purpose determines what details you need.

- **Villains** need drives, obstacles they face, and resources they can deploy against the party.
- **Allies** need reasons to help, limits on what they will do, and costs for their assistance.
- **Resources** need clear mechanics for what they provide and what they cost.
- **Verisimilitude NPCs** need to feel like part of the world without demanding attention.
- **Information sources** need to know things the party needs and reasons to share or withhold that knowledge.

**Step 2: Define what they want.** Everybody wants something. Understanding what the NPC wants is essential. If you do not know what an NPC wants, they are inert.

**Step 3: Define their obstacles.** Something is always blocking an NPC from achieving their goals. Heroes stand in the way of villains. The villain stands in the way of peace. Obstacles motivate NPCs to take action.

**Step 4: Define what they are doing about it.** Passive NPCs are boring. If the dark lord is just sitting in a tower brooding, they might as well not exist. NPCs should be actively working to achieve their goals, which means their actions impact the world whether the party intervenes or not.

**Step 5: Define their resources.** What tools does the NPC use to overcome obstacles? Money, minions, magical items, political connections, information, physical strength? The resource list determines what the NPC can offer or threaten.

### 4.2 Villain-Specific Additions

Villains who will recur need two additional elements:

**Moral Ambiguity:** The most memorable villains are not purely evil. They have understandable motivations, tragic flaws, or genuine virtues that coexist with their villainy. A villain who loves their family, keeps their word, or genuinely believes their terrible actions serve a greater good is more interesting than one who is evil for its own sake.

**Escalation Pattern:** How does the villain respond to setbacks? Do they become more desperate, more dangerous, more subtle? A villain who adapts to the party's tactics and returns with new strategies feels like a genuine threat rather than a recurring obstacle.

## 5. Reuse the Same Format When an NPC Grows

If the apothecary suddenly matters for six sessions, add details under the same headings instead of starting over. That way the page grows from play instead of from your anxiety.

### 5.1 The Living NPC Document

Maintain a single document for each recurring NPC. When new information emerges during play, add it under the appropriate heading. If you created an NPC with the five-line method and they become important, expand to the Drive/Threat/Dilemma/Conflict framework. If they become central to the campaign, add the Purpose framework's full detail.

**Example growth:**

*Session 1 (Five-Line Method):*
- Wants: Sell information about the guard rotations
- Hiding: Works for the thieves' guild as an informant
- Sounds like: Speaks in short, clipped sentences, as if every word costs money
- Can offer: Guard schedules, escape routes, introductions to the guild
- Difficulty: Demands payment upfront and will not negotiate

*Session 3 (Expanded):*
- Added Drive/Threat/Dilemma/Conflict
- Drive: Earn enough to buy his sister out of indentured servitude
- Threat: The guild suspects he is selling information to multiple buyers
- Dilemma: He can betray the guild to the authorities and save his sister, but the guild will kill his contacts
- Conflict: The party wants him to introduce them to the guild leader, but doing so would expose his side business

*Session 6 (Full Purpose Framework):*
- Added resources, escalation pattern, moral ambiguity
- Resources: Network of street informants, savings hidden in three locations, a forged identity document
- Escalation: When pressured, becomes more desperate and willing to take bigger risks
- Moral complexity: Genuinely cares for his sister but has hurt others to protect her; not evil, but compromised

This growth happened naturally, driven by what the players engaged with and what the story required. You did not need to create all of this in advance.

## 6. Vocal Distinction and Physicality

### 6.1 The Memory Problem

The most common NPC failure mode is not bad writing. It is that all NPCs sound the same at the table. If your merchant, guard captain, and ancient dragon all speak in your normal voice, your players will struggle to remember who is who.

Vocal distinction serves a practical purpose: it reduces cognitive load for your players. When they hear the rasping voice, they know it is the dockmaster without you needing to say so.

### 6.2 Vocal Techniques

You do not need to be a voice actor. Simple distinctions work:
- **Pitch:** Higher or lower than your normal voice
- **Pace:** Faster, slower, or with deliberate pauses
- **Volume:** Louder, softer, or varying unpredictably
- **Pattern:** Questions instead of statements, no contractions, repetition, constant hedging
- **Physicality:** A character who clears their throat constantly, one who clicks their tongue while thinking, one who never makes eye contact

Choose one vocal quality per NPC and stick with it. If you try to do too much, you will forget your own distinctions mid-session.

### 6.3 Physical Presence

How an NPC moves and occupies space is as distinctive as how they speak:
- Do they stand too close or keep distance?
- Do they fidget, stand perfectly still, or move with deliberate precision?
- Do they touch people while talking or avoid physical contact?
- Do they occupy the center of a room or stay near exits?

These physical choices help you roleplay the character and give players additional cues to remember them by.

## 7. The Three Stages of NPC Roleplaying

Roleplaying Tips identifies three stages of NPC portrayal. Understanding where you are on this spectrum helps you improve deliberately rather than feeling inadequate for not doing everything at once.

### 7.1 Stage 1: Think Like the NPC

In this stage, you describe actions in third person. You do not pressure yourself into voices or dramatic portrayal. You think about what the NPC does, how they react, what motivates them, and how they perceive the PCs.

A sticky note on your GM screen with the following questions helps:
> "If you were this NPC, who are you, why do you want what you want, and how would you react to what is happening right now?"

Mastery at this stage goes beyond thinking like the NPC. You learn to think like the NPC in the context of an amazing story. What would be interesting? What would create tension, humor, or pathos?

### 7.2 Stage 2: Distinct Voice and Mannerisms

In this stage, you add vocal distinction and physicality. The NPC sounds and moves differently from you and from other NPCs. You are not performing. You are distinguishing.

The goal is not theatrical perfection. The goal is that your players can tell, from voice alone, which NPC is speaking.

### 7.3 Stage 3: Full Character Portrayal

In this stage, you are fully inhabiting the character. You react as they would react, not as you would react or as the story demands. This level of immersion is not necessary for every NPC. Reserve it for the characters who matter most.

The danger of Stage 3 is that it can overshadow the players. A brilliantly performed NPC who dominates every scene becomes a problem, not an asset. The best NPCs, however well-portrayed, exist to spotlight the player characters, not themselves.

## 8. NPC Relationship Mapping

### 8.1 The Value of Connections

NPCs become more memorable and the world becomes more coherent when NPCs are connected to each other. A merchant who is the estranged brother of the guard captain creates more story than either character alone.

When creating a new NPC, consider connecting them to an existing NPC:
- Family relationships (siblings, parents, children, spouses)
- Professional relationships (partners, rivals, mentors, students)
- Social relationships (friends, enemies, debtors, creditors)
- Secret relationships (conspirators, lovers, blackmailer and victim)

These connections create a web that players can investigate, exploit, or become entangled in.

### 8.2 The Relationship Web Method

For a location with multiple NPCs (a city, a ship, a guild), create a simple relationship web:

1. List all NPCs
2. Draw a line between any two NPCs who know each other
3. Label each line with the nature of the relationship
4. Add one secret relationship that no one publicly acknowledges
5. Add one relationship that appears to be one thing but is actually another

This web becomes your source of complications, alliances, betrayals, and revelations. When the party interacts with one NPC, the web tells you how other NPCs will react.

## 9. The Warning: Lore vs. Readiness

Do not confuse lore with readiness. A paragraph about a character's tragic schooling does less for the next session than a note that says "owes money to the guard captain and hates being interrupted."

### 9.1 The Readiness Test

For any piece of NPC preparation, ask: "Will I use this in the next session?" If the answer is no, it is lore, not prep. Lore has its place in campaign bibles and world documents. Prep belongs on your session notes.

### 9.2 Just-in-Time Detail

Add details when they become relevant, not before. If the party visits the NPC's hometown, that is the moment to decide what the hometown is like. If the party investigates the NPC's past, that is the moment to invent the past. Preparing these details in advance wastes time and constrains your options.

This approach, sometimes called "just-in-time prep" or "draw maps, leave blanks" (from the Dungeon World SRD, CC-BY), gives you maximum flexibility while still providing solid foundations.

## 10. Quick Reference: NPC Creation by Importance

| Importance | Method | Prep Time | Detail Level |
|------------|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Background flavor (crowd, passerby) | Name + one adjective | 10 seconds | None |
| Minor NPC (shopkeeper, guard) | Five-line method | 2 minutes | 5 lines |
| Supporting NPC (recurring ally/rival) | 3-Line NPC + Hook | 1 minute | 3 lines |
| Major NPC (drives story arcs) | Drive/Threat/Dilemma/Conflict | 10 minutes | 4 detailed elements |
| Central NPC (campaign-defining) | Full Purpose framework | 20 minutes | All elements plus growth path |
