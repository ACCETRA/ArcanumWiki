A good session zero is not a ceremony. It is maintenance before the campaign has anything expensive to break. Most campaigns that fail do not fail because of bad plots or weak encounters. They fail because the people at the table wanted different things and no one knew it until the friction became damage.

This guide is built from open-source community knowledge, Creative Commons licensed materials, and freely available safety frameworks. It is designed to be comprehensive enough that you can run a complete session zero from this document alone, or extract the pieces that fit your particular group and table culture.

---

## 1. The Campaign Promise

Tell the group what kind of game you are offering in one to two sentences. This is not a marketing pitch. It is a commitment. If you cannot describe your campaign in two sentences, you do not know what you are offering yet.

**Examples of clear campaign promises:**
- "This is a frontier wilderness campaign where resources are tight, travel is dangerous, and the wilderness itself is the primary adversary."
- "This is an urban intrigue campaign set in a city where every faction has competing interests and combat will often have social consequences."
- "This is a horror campaign where characters may die or be permanently changed, and the primary emotional beats are dread and desperate hope."
- "This is a heroic fantasy campaign where the party will become powerful, face epic threats, and the tone is generally optimistic."

The campaign promise serves two critical functions. First, it gives players the information they need to create characters that fit the table's goals. A player who wants tactical combat will be disappointed in a purely political campaign, and a player who wants deep political intrigue may feel lost in a pure dungeon crawl. Second, it becomes the anchor you return to when decisions get complicated. When you are unsure whether a particular subplot fits, ask whether it serves the campaign promise. When a player asks whether a particular character concept works, measure it against the promise.

**Open-source resource:** This technique is adapted from Michael E. Shea's "Lazy GM's Resource Document" (SlyFlourish.com, CC-BY-4.0), which emphasizes beginning campaign prep by focusing on what the table wants to experience.

## 2. Safety Tools and Consent Frameworks

Safety tools are not a sign that you expect something to go wrong. They are a sign that you care enough to prevent it. Every table benefits from safety tools, regardless of how well the players know each other. In fact, groups of close friends sometimes need them most, because familiarity can breed assumptions about what others are comfortable with.

### 2.1 Lines and Veils

Lines and Veils, originally developed for tabletop RPGs and now widely adopted across the hobby, establish content boundaries before play begins.

- **A Line** is a hard limit. Content behind a line does not appear in the game at all. Lines are non-negotiable boundaries. Common lines include sexual assault, child harm, detailed torture, suicide, self-harm, and specific phobias held by players. The only person who decides what their line is, is the person whose line it is. The table does not vote on lines.
- **A Veil** is a soft limit. Content behind a veil can exist in the story but is not described in detail. When a veil is drawn, the game "fades to black" and skips past the detailed content. Common veils include sexual content, graphic violence, harm to animals, and body horror. Unlike lines, veiled content can be referenced as happening off-screen.
- **"Ask First"** is an additional category worth establishing. Some content may be acceptable to include but only if discussed beforehand. This gives players agency over when potentially sensitive material enters the story.
- **"Yes Please"** is a category for content players actively want. While most safety frameworks focus on exclusion, it is equally valuable to know what players are enthusiastic about including.

**How to implement Lines and Veils at your table:**

1. Before session zero, prepare a list of common topics that might come up in your campaign based on its genre and tone. A horror campaign's list will look different from a heroic fantasy campaign's list.
2. During session zero, distribute the list and give each player time to mark topics as Line, Veil, Ask First, or Yes Please.
3. Collect the lists privately. The GM should be the only person who sees everyone's boundaries, to protect player privacy.
4. Compile a master list of all lines and share it with the table as a collective agreement. Do not reveal who marked what. The master list should contain every line anyone marked.
5. Review veils and ask-first items. These require the GM to remember when to fade to black or check in.
6. Keep the compiled list accessible during play. A sticky note on your GM screen, a note in your digital setup, or a printed card works well.

### 2.2 The X-Card, N-Card, and O-Card

The X-Card system, created by John Stavropoulos, is an in-game safety tool that allows any participant to immediately stop or alter content that makes them uncomfortable. It is simple, effective, and works across all play contexts including online games.

- **The X-Card:** Any player or the GM can tap the X-card (or type "X" in chat for online play) at any time when content becomes uncomfortable. When the X-card is played, the current content is immediately stopped, rewound, or skipped. No explanation is required. No one asks why. The group simply moves on. The simplicity and lack of required justification is what makes the X-card powerful.
- **The N-Card (or Yellow Card):** A participant can play the N-card to indicate "I am uncomfortable with where this is going, but I do not want to stop entirely." It signals that the content should proceed carefully, or that the participant needs a moment. This provides a middle ground between full stop and continuing unabated.
- **The O-Card (or Green Card):** A participant can play the O-card to indicate "I am enjoying this content and want more of it." This is particularly valuable because it gives positive feedback about what is working, not just negative feedback about what is not.

**For online play:** The X/N/O system works perfectly in digital environments. Most VTTs have a card system, or players can simply type X, N, or O in the chat. The GM should explicitly introduce these tools at the start of the session and model their use by keeping their own cards visible.

**Open-source resource:** The TTRPG Safety Toolkit, available freely from RPG community sites, includes printable X/N/O card templates and detailed implementation guides.

### 2.3 Monte Cook RPG Consent Checklist

The Monte Cook Games Consent Checklist (available as a free PDF from their website) provides a structured, detailed approach to pre-game boundary setting. It covers a wide variety of topics including physical violence, sexual content, body horror, phobias, harm to children, harm to animals, natural disasters, and many more.

The checklist uses a color-coded system:
- **Green (Yes/Consent):** Enthusiastic consent to include this content
- **Yellow (Maybe/Veil):** Okay if handled carefully, off-screen, or with fade-to-black
- **Red (No/Hard Line):** Do not include this content

**Best practices for using the Consent Checklist:**

1. Distribute the checklist before session zero, ideally at least a few days in advance, so players have time to think through their responses without pressure.
2. Make clear that filling out the checklist is mandatory for participation. This normalizes it as a standard part of joining the campaign, not an optional extra step.
3. Collect responses privately. Only the GM should see individual responses.
4. Compile a unified set of red (line) items to share with the table.
5. Note yellow items and be prepared to handle them with care during play.
6. Revisit the checklist periodically, especially if the campaign shifts in tone or if new players join.

**Open-source resource:** Monte Cook Games publishes "Consent in Gaming" as a free PDF download, suitable for any RPG system.

### 2.4 The Luxton Technique

The Luxton Technique involves an open discussion between everyone at the table about potential trauma triggers. Unlike the checklist approach, this is a conversational method where the group discusses boundaries together.

The key principle is that it is not possible to identify every trigger in advance, and there is no social pressure to go into details or provide justification for a boundary. Someone saying "I would prefer we don't include spiders" is sufficient. The group accepts the boundary and moves on.

### 2.5 Stars and Wishes

Stars and Wishes, created by Brie Sheldon, is a feedback tool used at the end of sessions rather than during play. Each player shares:
- A **Star:** Something they enjoyed during the session
- A **Wish:** Something they would like more of, less of, or done differently

This creates an ongoing feedback loop that catches concerns before they become serious problems. It also reinforces what is working well, which helps the GM understand what the table values.

**Implementation tips for Stars and Wishes:**
- Use it at the end of every session for the first few months of a campaign, then check in periodically.
- The GM should go first to model vulnerability and openness to feedback.
- Write down the wishes and review them before the next session. If a wish is feasible to incorporate, do so and acknowledge it. If a wish conflicts with other table needs or the campaign promise, discuss it openly.
- Stars and Wishes is not just for problems. When a player gives a star, they are telling you what to keep doing. That information is as valuable as the wishes.

### 2.6 Normalizing Safety Tool Use

The most important element of any safety tool is that its use is normalized and supported. Safety tools fail when players feel they will be judged for using them, or when the GM treats them as a formality to check off rather than a genuine commitment to care.

**Practices that normalize safety tools:**
- The GM should model using safety tools themselves. If the GM draws a veil or taps an X-card, it signals that these tools belong to everyone at the table.
- Practice using the tools during session zero before any real content is at stake. Run a brief hypothetical scene and demonstrate how the X-card works.
- Never ask why someone used a safety tool. The reason belongs to them. Simply acknowledge and move on.
- Revisit safety tools periodically. Boundaries change, and what was comfortable in session one may not be comfortable in session ten.
- If a new player joins mid-campaign, run a mini session zero with the full group to re-establish boundaries with the new dynamic.

**Open-source resource:** Script Change (by Beau Jágr Sheldon) is a multi-function content, consent, and safety toolbox for collaborative creation and gameplay, available under Creative Commons license. It includes the "Rewind," "Fast Forward," and "Pause" mechanics that function like media controls for your game.

## 3. Practical Rules and Table Logistics

Cover the questions that create friction later. If you leave these vague, the campaign ends up negotiating them in the worst possible moment.

### 3.1 Scheduling and Attendance

**Meeting frequency and duration:** How often do you meet, and for how long? Be specific. "Weekly for three hours" is a commitment everyone can plan around. "Whenever we feel like it" leads to the campaign dissolving.

**Attendance policy:** What happens when someone misses a session? Common approaches include:
- The character is "present but passive" and does not take actions or face consequences.
- The character is "off-screen" and the GM provides a brief narrative explanation.
- The character is run by another player or the GM (with the absent player's consent).
- The session is rescheduled if more than one player cannot attend.

Decide this in advance. The policy should be written down and shared with everyone.

**Late arrivals and early departures:** How late is too late? If a player arrives 45 minutes into a 3-hour session, do they join mid-scene? If someone needs to leave early, how do you handle their character's exit?

### 3.2 Technology and Distractions

**Phone and device policy:** Some tables ban devices entirely for focus and immersion. Others encourage their use for rule lookups, digital character sheets, and dice rolling. Decide as a group and enforce consistently. The GM should follow the same rules as the players.

**Online play logistics:** If playing online, establish:
- Which platform you are using (Discord, Zoom, Roll20, Foundry, etc.)
- Whether cameras are on or optional
- Whether players should mute when not speaking
- How you will handle technical difficulties
- A backup plan if the primary platform fails

### 3.3 Rules Handling

**House rules:** If you are modifying core rules, communicate these changes before the campaign begins. Common house rules include milestone leveling versus experience points, healing potions as bonus actions, critical hit and fumble tables, homebrew spells or abilities, and modified death saving throw rules.

Write down every house rule. Do not rely on memory. Memory drifts, and what you remember saying may not be what players remember hearing.

**Rules disputes mid-session:** Decide how you will handle disagreements about rules during play. Common approaches include:
- The GM makes a ruling in the moment and looks up the correct rule after the session.
- The player accepts the GM's ruling for the session, and the group discusses it before the next session.
- A designated "rules lawyer" player looks up the rule while play continues.

The key is having a process. Sly Flourish recommends writing down rules questions during the session and looking them up after the game, making a ruling during play to keep things moving. This approach, from the Lazy GM's Resource Document (CC-BY-4.0), prioritizes pacing over precision.

### 3.4 Player Versus Player (PvP)

Player versus player actions require explicit discussion. Can players lie to each other, steal from each other, engage in combat, or work at cross-purposes? Some groups find internal tension adds drama. Others consider it disruptive.

If you allow PvP actions, establish clear boundaries:
- Is deception between player characters acceptable?
- Is physical combat between player characters acceptable?
- Is theft between player characters acceptable?
- Must both players consent out-of-character before PvP occurs?

The most important principle is that no player should be surprised by PvP. If PvP is possible at your table, everyone must understand it and agree to it before the campaign begins.

### 3.5 Food, Drink, and Social Norms

Decide whether food and drink are welcome at the table, whether you will take breaks during the session, and what the social expectations are. Some tables prefer a focused game session; others treat the game as a social occasion with extended breaks for meals and conversation. Both approaches are valid. The key is matching expectations.

## 4. Character Creation with the Campaign in Mind

Ask each player to answer three questions before they finalize their character. These answers create momentum. They also stop the common problem where everyone arrives with a dramatic backstory and no reason to trust anyone else.

### 4.1 The Three Binding Questions

1. **Why is this character willing to work with the group?** Every character needs a reason to be part of an adventuring party. "We all met in a tavern" is not a reason. Valid reasons include shared enemies, mutual goals, financial necessity, family obligations, religious duty, personal debt, or genuine friendship. The reason does not need to be dramatic. It needs to be functional.

2. **What does this character want in the first ten sessions?** Short-term goals give the GM material to work with immediately. A goal of "become the greatest swordsman in the realm" is too vague. A goal of "find the man who killed my mentor" is actionable. A goal of "earn enough gold to buy back my family's farm" gives the GM a clear motivation to tie into quests and rewards.

3. **What part of the world already knows this character's name?** This question connects the character to the setting before play begins. Perhaps they are wanted in one city, revered in their hometown, or indebted to a specific guild. These connections become hooks the GM can pull on. They also give the character a sense of existing in the world rather than being dropped into it as a blank slate.

### 4.2 Inter-Character Connections

After each player has answered the three questions, facilitate a round of inter-character connection building. Each player should establish at least one connection to another character in the party. These connections can be:
- Shared history ("We served together in the border wars.")
- Mutual obligation ("You saved my life in the sewers beneath Thornwall.")
- Complementary goals ("We're both searching for the same lost city, but for different reasons.")
- Personal bonds ("You're the only person who knows my real identity.")

These connections transform a group of strangers into a party with existing relationships. They also give the GM relationship dynamics to play with.

### 4.3 Integrating Backstories

Discuss how backstories will be used in the campaign. Make clear that you will integrate backstory elements into the campaign, but that you cannot promise every backstory element will receive equal spotlight time. Be honest about your capacity as a GM.

Sly Flourish's "Review the Characters" step (from the Lazy GM's 8 Steps, CC-BY-4.0) recommends that the GM ask: "What hook can I include in the next session to draw this character into the game?" This question, asked for each character, ensures that backstories are not just written documents but active forces in the campaign.

## 5. Campaign Tone and Content Calibration

### 5.1 Tone Check

Discuss the emotional tone of the campaign. Is it gritty and dangerous, or heroic and optimistic? Is it morally gray or clearly defined? Is humor welcome or does it break immersion? What is the ratio of combat to exploration to social interaction? What genre conventions are you embracing and which are you rejecting?

**A useful exercise:** Each player names one movie, book, or game that represents the kind of story they want to experience. The GM does the same. Compare the answers. If everyone names "The Lord of the Rings" except one player who names "Game of Thrones," you have a calibration issue to discuss.

### 5.2 Expectation Alignment

Discuss what success looks like in your campaign. Are characters expected to become heroes of legend, or are they ordinary people trying to survive? Will the campaign reach a definitive conclusion, or could it run indefinitely? What happens if a character dies?

Also discuss what player behavior you expect. Is note-taking encouraged? Should players track their own resources? Is out-of-character strategic discussion allowed during combat? Is "metagaming" (using player knowledge the character wouldn't have) something the group cares about?

## 6. The Written Recap

End session zero with a short written document. If it matters enough to agree on, it matters enough to survive memory.

The written recap should include:
- The campaign promise (1-2 sentences)
- The agreed lines and any shared veils (without attributing them to individuals)
- All house rules
- The attendance policy
- The PvP policy
- The device/technology policy
- Each character's name, class, and one-line summary
- Each character's answer to the three binding questions
- The inter-character connections
- The meeting schedule (day, time, frequency)
- Any other agreements made during session zero

Distribute this document to all players and keep it accessible. Refer back to it when questions arise. Update it as the campaign evolves and new agreements are made.

**Open-source resource:** The TTRPG Kickstarter Budgeting Tool by Ash Kreider and various community session planning templates provide structured formats for tracking campaign agreements and player information.

## 7. Post-Session Zero Check-Ins

Session zero begins the dialogue; it does not solve every problem. Plan check-ins throughout your campaign to revisit agreements and address emerging concerns.

- **After session one:** A quick group check-in to confirm everyone is still comfortable with the tone and content. This is a good time to adjust boundaries that were theoretical in session zero but feel different in actual play.
- **Monthly Stars and Wishes:** Use the Stars and Wishes feedback method at the end of a session once per month.
- **After major campaign events:** If the campaign includes intense emotional content, check in with players individually after the session.
- **When new players join:** Run a condensed session zero with the new player and the existing group.

## 8. Quick Reference: Session Zero Agenda

Use this as your session zero running order. Allocate time for each section based on your group's needs. A full session zero typically takes 2-4 hours.

| Section | Time | Key Activities |
|---------|------|---------------|
| Welcome and introductions | 10 min | Introduce yourself, confirm all players are present, set a comfortable tone |
| Campaign promise | 10 min | Present the 1-2 sentence campaign description and confirm everyone is interested |
| Safety tools | 20-30 min | Distribute and collect Consent Checklists, explain Lines/Veils, practice X/N/O cards |
| Table rules | 20 min | Schedule, attendance, devices, rules disputes, PvP policy |
| Character creation | 45-60 min | Three binding questions, inter-character connections, backstory integration |
| Tone calibration | 15 min | Genre references, tone discussion, success definitions |
| Written recap | 15 min | Compile agreements, distribute to all players |
| Buffer | 15 min | Unstructured time for player questions and social connection |
