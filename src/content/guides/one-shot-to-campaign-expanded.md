The easiest way to force a campaign is to ignore what the players actually found interesting in the first session. Most failed campaign conversions die because the GM had a predetermined idea of where the story "should" go, and that destination had nothing to do with what made the one-shot work.

This guide synthesizes techniques from Dungeon World's fronts system, Blades in the Dark's faction clocks, Sly Flourish's Lazy GM campaign building, and the Alexandrian's node-based scenario design to create a method for genuine campaign growth that follows the table's energy rather than imposing a predetermined plot.

---

## 1. Start with the Residue

After the one-shot, ask what remains unresolved. This is not about what you, as the GM, planned to be unresolved. It is about what the players actually engaged with, asked about, reacted to, or remembered.

### 1.1 Identifying Campaign Seeds

The following questions help surface the residue that becomes your campaign foundation:

- **Who escaped?** If an enemy fled during the one-shot, that escape is a living thread. The players will remember the enemy who got away. That enemy is now a recurring threat with a grudge, a network, and time to prepare.
- **Which location felt bigger than one night?** If the players explored a dungeon and you described deeper levels they never reached, those levels are a campaign seed. If they visited a city and only saw one district, the rest of the city is unexplored territory full of potential.
- **Which NPC got more attention than expected?** The NPCs players ask about, joke about, or go out of their way to interact with are the NPCs they care about. A tavern keeper who made an impression is worth more than the quest-giver you spent hours preparing. An ally who helped the party creates a relationship that can be tested and developed.
- **What question did the players keep asking?** Player questions reveal what they are curious about. "Who built this ruin?" "What was that symbol?" "Why was the villain collecting those artifacts?" Each question is a door the players are asking you to open.
- **What did the players create that you did not plan?** If players invented a detail, an organization, a rumor, or a backstory element during play, that creation belongs to them. Using it makes the world feel responsive to their actions.
- **What choice had consequences?** If the players chose one path over another, the unchosen path did not disappear. It went on without them. If they allied with the thieves' guild, the merchant guild they ignored is now their rival. If they killed the bandit leader, the bandit's second-in-command may be rallying the survivors.

### 1.2 The Residue Inventory

After your one-shot, create a written inventory of residue. List every thread, question, location, NPC, and unresolved choice. Do this as soon as possible after the session while memories are fresh. Your list might look like this:

| Category | Item | Player Engagement Level | Campaign Potential |
|----------|------|------------------------|-------------------|
| NPC | Vara the fence, who helped the party escape | High - players laughed at her jokes, asked her name | Ally or information broker; knows the underworld |
| Location | The sealed door on level 3 of the dungeon | High - players spent 10 minutes trying to open it | Future adventure site; what's behind it? |
| Enemy | The cult leader who teleported away | High - players cursed when he escaped | Recurring villain; now knows their capabilities |
| Question | "What was that purple flame?" | Medium - asked twice during play | Setting mystery; source of strange magic |
| Choice | Party spared the goblin prisoners | Medium - debated before deciding | Goblins may become allies or future complication |
| Player Creation | A player named the tavern "The Crooked Flagon" | High - everyone started using the name | Persistent location in the campaign world |

This inventory becomes your campaign's first front. You are not inventing new hooks. You are following threads that already exist.

## 2. Promote Only One Thing at a Time

If you turn every loose end into a future arc, the story becomes a pile of hooks instead of a direction. Pick the single thread with the most energy and let the rest stay background until they earn more room.

### 2.1 The Single Thread Principle

A campaign needs focus. When a player looks back on ten sessions and tries to describe what the campaign is about, they should be able to answer in one sentence. If the answer is "Well, we're dealing with the escaped cult leader, and also the political situation in the city, and there's something about the sealed door, and the goblins keep showing up, and..." then you have promoted too many threads.

Choose one thread to be the current focus. The others remain present but secondary. They appear as rumors, background events, and occasional complications rather than competing main plots.

**How to choose the focus thread:**
- Which thread generated the most player energy during the one-shot?
- Which thread connects to the most characters' goals and backstories?
- Which thread are you, as GM, most excited to develop?
- Which thread has the clearest next step?

The answers to these questions usually point to the same thread. If they point to different threads, discuss with your players. Ask them which thread they are most interested in pursuing. Their answer is your focus.

### 2.2 Background Threads as Living Elements

Just because a thread is not the current focus does not mean it is frozen. Background threads should evolve slightly between sessions. The sealed door on level 3 does not need its own adventure yet, but someone else might be interested in it. The goblins the party spared might send a messenger with a gift. These small touches keep the world alive without competing for focus.

The Dungeon World fronts system handles this elegantly through "grim portents." Each background thread has a sequence of events that will occur if the players do not intervene. You advance one portent occasionally, keeping the thread alive without requiring a full adventure. The Alexandrian's node-based design similarly lets you maintain "proactive nodes" that occasionally reach out to the party with information or complications.

## 3. Build Campaign Fronts Using the Dungeon World Model

Dungeon World's "fronts" system, available in the Dungeon World SRD (Creative Commons license), is one of the most powerful tools for campaign building. A front is a collection of linked dangers with a shared direction. Think of it as a sinister campaign moving in parallel to the actions of the PCs.

### 3.1 Types of Fronts

There are two kinds of fronts:

- **Adventure Fronts** are immediate, session-to-session threats. They contain dangers the party will deal with over a few sessions. Think of them as episodes in a television series.
- **Campaign Fronts** are the larger, season-spanning threats that unify your entire campaign. They burn slower but have bigger scope and deeper impact. The adventure fronts connect to and feed the campaign front.

When converting a one-shot to a campaign, your residue inventory becomes your first set of fronts. The most energetic thread becomes your first adventure front. The larger implications of that thread become your campaign front.

### 3.2 The Components of a Front

Each front contains the following elements:

**Dangers (2-3 per adventure front, 3-5 per campaign front):** The specific threats, factions, or challenges within the front. Each danger has:
- An **impulse** (what it wants)
- **Grim portents** (what will happen if unchecked)
- An **impending doom** (the final consequence)

**Grim Portents (1-3 for adventure fronts, 3-5 for campaign fronts):** These are the steps your dangers follow as they move forward. They represent the countdown. If the cult leader escaped in your one-shot, his grim portents might be:
1. The cult regroups at a hidden shrine
2. The cult recruits from the desperate and displaced
3. The cult performs a ritual that destabilizes the local ley lines
4. The cult leader unlocks the power of the artifact he stole
5. The cult's actions attract the attention of a greater power

**Impending Doom:** What happens if the danger is not stopped. This should be serious enough to get the players' attention but not so dire that they have no choice but to tackle it. "The city falls to chaos" is serious. "The world ends" removes player agency.

### 3.3 Fronts Example: From One-Shot to Campaign

Let us say your one-shot involved the party raiding a cult's ritual site and stopping a summoning. The cult leader escaped with a partial ritual. Here is how that becomes a front:

**Campaign Front: The Broken Seal**
*The cult of the Ebon Veil nearly succeeded in opening a gate to the void. Though the party stopped the initial ritual, the cult leader escaped with knowledge that could complete the summoning. The barrier between worlds is weakening, and other forces have noticed.*

**Danger 1: The Ebon Veil Cult**
- Impulse: To complete the ritual and open the gate
- Grim Portents: (1) The cult regroups at their hidden sanctuary, (2) They recruit new members from the desperate, (3) They acquire the three components needed for the complete ritual, (4) The cult leader begins the final summoning
- Impending Doom: A tear in reality opens, unleashing void entities into the material world

**Danger 2: The Vigilant Order (rival faction)**
- Impulse: To destroy all knowledge of the void, including witnesses
- Grim Portents: (1) Vigilant agents arrive in the region, (2) They begin interrogating and eliminating those who know about the cult, (3) They declare the party a threat to be neutralized
- Impending Doom: The Vigilant Order seizes control of the region through martial law

**Danger 3: The Corruption Spreading from the Ritual Site**
- Impulse: To grow and consume
- Grim Portents: (1) Animals near the ritual site become aggressive and mutated, (2) Local vegetation dies and turns black, (3) Reality near the site becomes unstable
- Impending Doom: The corrupted zone expands into a blight that threatens the entire region

This single front gives you material for multiple adventures: dealing with the cult's recruitment, retrieving the ritual components before the cult does, surviving Vigilant Order interference, investigating the spreading corruption, and ultimately confronting the cult leader before the final summoning.

**Open-source resource:** The Dungeon World SRD (CC-BY) contains the complete fronts system with examples and danger types including Ambitious Organizations, Planar Forces, Arcane Enemies, Hordes, and Cursed Places.

## 4. Use Faction Clocks to Create a Living World

Blades in the Dark's faction clocks (available in the Blades in the Dark SRD) are a technique for making the world dynamic. Each faction has a long-term goal represented by a "clock" divided into segments. When the PCs have downtime, or when the narrative allows, the GM ticks forward the faction clocks. In this way, the world around the PCs evolves whether they are directly involved or not.

### 4.1 Setting Up Faction Clocks

For your campaign conversion, identify 3-5 factions connected to your residue threads. Each faction gets a clock with 4-8 segments representing their goal. Examples from our "Broken Seal" front:

| Faction | Goal | Clock (6 segments) | Connection to PCs |
|---------|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Ebon Veil Cult | Complete the void ritual | 1/6 filled | Primary antagonists from one-shot |
| Vigilant Order | Eliminate void knowledge | 0/6 filled | Potential allies or antagonists |
| Merchant Consortium | Profit from the instability | 0/6 filled | Could hire or betray the party |
| Goblins (spared by party) | Establish a safe territory | 0/6 filled | Potential allies the party created |
| Corrupted Entity (void being) | Enter the material world | 0/6 filled | Ultimate threat behind the cult |

### 4.2 Advancing Clocks

Faction clocks advance under specific conditions:
- When the PCs take downtime to rest or pursue personal goals, all active faction clocks advance by 1 segment.
- When the PCs directly interfere with a faction, that faction's clock may pause or reverse.
- When the PCs ignore a faction to pursue other goals, that faction's clock advances by 1-2 segments.
- When a faction achieves a significant victory or the PCs suffer a major setback, the relevant clock advances by 2 segments.

This creates genuine consequence for player choices. If the party spends three sessions dealing with a side quest while the cult's clock ticks forward, they will return to find the cult has grown stronger, recruited more members, or completed another step of their plan.

### 4.3 Tug-of-War Clocks

Some situations are not simple progress clocks. A tug-of-war clock can be filled and emptied by events. For example, the relationship between the party and the goblins they spared could be a tug-of-war clock. Positive interactions fill it; negative interactions or failures to follow through on promises empty it. When it fills completely, the goblins become steadfast allies. When it empties, they become enemies.

**Open-source resource:** Blades in the Dark SRD contains the complete faction clock system, including mission clocks, tug-of-war clocks, and long-term project clocks.

## 5. Keep the Original Tone Recognizable

If the one-shot worked because it was fast, eerie, and local, do not immediately bloat it into continent-wide politics. Let the campaign expand in the same language the table already liked.

### 5.1 The Spiral Expansion Method

The most natural way to expand a one-shot is in spirals, not jumps. Each ring of the spiral represents a larger scope, but you move through the rings gradually.

| Ring | Scope | Example |
|------|-------|---------|
| Center | The one-shot location | The dungeon where the cult's ritual took place |
| Ring 1 | The immediate surroundings | The village near the dungeon; local reactions to the cult's activity |
| Ring 2 | The region | The province; other towns affected; regional powers investigating |
| Ring 3 | The nation | The capital; political implications; national factions taking interest |
| Ring 4 | The continent/world | The void threat's cosmic significance; other seals around the world |

Start at the center and spend multiple sessions at each ring before moving outward. The table should feel like the world is opening up naturally, not like you teleported them to a different campaign.

### 5.2 Tone Consistency Checklist

Before adding a new element to your expanding campaign, check it against the one-shot's tone:
- Would this element feel out of place in the one-shot?
- Does it preserve the emotional register that made the one-shot work?
- If the one-shot was gritty and the new element is heroic, is there a bridge between them?
- If the one-shot was local and the new element is political, how does the transition feel earned?

If an element fails the tone check, either modify it to fit or save it for later when the campaign has naturally evolved toward that register.

## 6. Give the Party a Reason to Stay Together

One-shots often work on coincidence. The characters meet in a tavern, share a single adventure, and go their separate ways. Campaigns usually cannot sustain coincidence. Before session two, make sure the group has a shared problem, employer, debt, promise, or enemy strong enough to survive logistics and bad rolls.

### 6.1 Shared Anchors

An anchor is a reason the party continues to operate as a unit. Effective anchors include:

- **A shared enemy:** The escaped cult leader knows the party's faces and will come for them. This mutual threat requires mutual defense.
- **A shared debt:** The party owes someone powerful who expects them to continue working together. Failure has consequences.
- **A shared promise:** They made a commitment to an NPC, to each other, or to an ideal. Promises are powerful motivators when tested.
- **A shared secret:** They know something dangerous that binds them together. If one falls, the others are compromised.
- **A shared institution:** They belong to an organization that assigns them missions. This is the most explicit anchor and works well for some campaigns, though it can feel restrictive for others.
- **A shared location:** They are invested in a particular place and its protection. This works especially well with the spiral method, as the location is already the center of your campaign.

### 6.2 Testing the Anchor

Whatever anchor you choose, it should be strong enough to survive:
- A character death (will the remaining party still have reason to stay together?)
- A betrayal or moral disagreement
- A long period without adventure (why don't they just go home?)
- A personal emergency for one character (can the others help without breaking their own commitments?)

If your anchor cannot survive these tests, strengthen it or add a secondary anchor before the campaign progresses too far.

## 7. End by Naming the Next Horizon

The first campaign session after a one-shot should end with a very clear next destination. Momentum matters more than lore at that point.

### 7.1 The Three-Option Close

Sly Flourish (CC-BY-4.0) recommends ending sessions by offering three clear options and letting players choose their path. This creates investment because the choice belongs to the players, not the GM. The options should be:
- Relevant to the current front
- Meaningfully different from each other
- Equally viable (none should be obviously the "right" choice)
- Connected to different player motivations

For example, after the first post-one-shot session dealing with the cult's regrouping, you might offer:
1. "The cult's recruitment is centered in the Dock Ward. You could infiltrate their operations there."
2. "The Vigilant Order has set up headquarters in an abandoned temple. You could approach them for an alliance against the cult."
3. "The ritual site is still spreading corruption. You could return to investigate what's causing it."

Each option points to a different thread within your front, letting the players direct the campaign's focus while you maintain the overall structure.

### 7.2 Session Ending Techniques

The best pacing tool is often where you stop. End after a reveal, a clean victory, or the moment before a dangerous room opens. This gives your next session direction before anyone has time to drift.

Strong session endings include:
- **The cliffhanger:** End on a moment of danger or revelation. "As you open the door, you see a figure in Vigilant Order armor standing over a dead cultist. They turn to you, blade drawn."
- **The choice point:** End with the players making a significant decision that will have consequences. Let them discuss the choice between sessions.
- **The discovery:** End with the players learning something that recontextualizes what they know. "The cult leader's journal reveals that the ritual was not meant to summon something, but to keep something out."
- **The arrival:** End with something arriving that changes the situation. "As you celebrate your victory, a messenger arrives from the capital bearing the royal seal and a summons."

**Open-source resource:** Sly Flourish's "Return of the Lazy Dungeon Master" and the Lazy GM's Resource Document (both CC-BY-4.0) contain extensive guidance on session endings, strong starts, and maintaining campaign momentum.

## 8. Node-Based Scenario Design for Campaign Flexibility

The Alexandrian's node-based scenario design (freely available at TheAlexandrian.net) provides a framework for building non-linear campaigns. In node-based design, "nodes" are Points of Interest: locations, people, organizations, events, or activities. Connections between nodes are clues, geography, or proactive elements.

### 8.1 The Five-Node Mystery

For each adventure within your campaign, use the five-node structure:

1. **The Hook / Opening Scene:** How the adventure begins. Often this is the scene from your previous session's ending.
2. **Three Cool Locations or People:** The core nodes. Each should be interesting enough that the players want to engage with it regardless of plot relevance.
3. **The Ending:** Where the adventure culminates. This might be a confrontation, a discovery, or a major choice.
4. **Connect them with clues:** Each node should have clues pointing to at least two other nodes.

### 8.2 The Three Clue Rule

For any conclusion you want the players to reach, include at least three clues. The underlying theory is that having three distinct options provides sufficient redundancy to create a robust scenario. Even if the PCs miss the first clue and misinterpret the second, the third clue provides a final safety net.

The inversion is equally valuable: **If the PCs have access to any three clues, they will reach at least one conclusion.** This means you can trust your players to find their way through your campaign without railroading.

**Open-source resource:** Justin Alexander's complete node-based scenario design series is freely available at TheAlexandrian.net, including the Three Clue Rule, node map examples, and the 5x5 node mystery campaign structure.

## 9. Maintaining Momentum Over the Long Term

### 9.1 The Strong Start

Every session should begin with something happening. The strong start draws players out of the real world and into the game. It does not need to be combat. It needs to be engaging. Examples:
- "A stranger approaches your table in the tavern and places a sealed letter before you. The wax seal bears a symbol you recognize from the ritual site."
- "You are awakened by smoke. The building next to your lodgings is on fire, and you can hear screams."
- "A member of the city guard arrives and arrests one of the party, citing evidence connecting them to the cult."

**Open-source resource:** Sly Flourish's "Starting Strong" (SlyFlourish.com, CC-BY-4.0) provides extensive guidance on session-opening techniques including cold opens and in-media-res starts.

### 9.2 Spotlight Rotation

Track which characters have had their moment in recent sessions. Use character backstories, goals, and relationships to create personalized spotlight moments. If one character's quest has dominated three sessions, design the next session to highlight someone else. The Ultimate Guide to Spotlight Time in TTRPGs (TTRPG-Games.com, community resource) offers detailed techniques for balancing player engagement.

### 9.3 Regular Reassessment

Every 3-4 sessions, review your fronts and clocks. Ask:
- Which threads are generating energy? Promote those.
- Which threads have gone cold? Let them fade or resolve them off-screen.
- Have new threads emerged from play? Incorporate them as new fronts.
- Are the faction clocks creating interesting pressure? If not, add more factions or tighten the clock pressure.
- Is the campaign still aligned with the original promise? If the tone has drifted, discuss with your players.

## 10. Quick Reference: Conversion Checklist

| Step | Action | Timeframe |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Immediately after one-shot | Create residue inventory from player engagement | Same day |
| Before session two | Choose focus thread; identify background threads | 1-2 days after |
| Before session two | Create first adventure front with 2-3 dangers | 2-3 days after |
| Before session two | Establish shared party anchor | During session two prep |
| Before session two | Set up 3-5 faction clocks | During session two prep |
| Session two | End with three clear options for next session | During play |
| After session two | Advance all relevant faction clocks | Same day |
| Ongoing | Review fronts and clocks every 3-4 sessions | Periodic |
