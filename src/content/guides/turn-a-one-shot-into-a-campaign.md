The easiest way to force a campaign is to ignore what the players actually found interesting in the first session.

## Start with the residue

After the one-shot, ask what remains unresolved:

- Who escaped?
- Which location felt bigger than one night?
- Which NPC got more attention than expected?
- What question did the players keep asking?

That residue is the campaign seed. Use it.

## Promote only one thing at a time

If you turn every loose end into a future arc, the story becomes a pile of hooks instead of a direction. Pick the single thread with the most energy and let the rest stay background until they earn more room.

## Keep the original tone recognizable

If the one-shot worked because it was fast, eerie, and local, do not immediately bloat it into continent-wide politics. Let the campaign expand in the same language the table already liked.

## Give the party a reason to stay together

One-shots often work on coincidence. Campaigns usually cannot. Before session two, make sure the group has a shared problem, employer, debt, promise, or enemy strong enough to survive logistics and bad rolls.

## End by naming the next horizon

The first campaign session after a one-shot should end with a very clear next destination. Momentum matters more than lore at that point.
